Activities

I earn my livelihood as an Internet programmer, and as such I attempt to help people finding their ways about on the web by implementing various kinds of search engines. I do this together with friends and colleagues at Lund University Libraries and before that at NetLab (see also info on NetLab's birthday party and a note on the history of NetLab).

Web indexing

Initially, I worked exclusively with robot based search indexes. Here at NetLab we implemented the combine harvesting robot, which you may get for free. The work is done as a part of the Desire project.

We used the Combine for a number of purposes. Among those were to build the Nordic Web Index. We released the NWI 1996, about a month after Digital released Alta Vista. Thus we were among the first search engines to support European character sets (with nasty characters like å, ä, ø, ö and the like). During its life time, NWI usually had a higher coverage of the Nordic web than its commercial competition. The NWI was also one of the very first web indexes that had extensive support for Dublin Core Metadata. We gave a presentation on this work at the 7th Internation World wide web conference.

For quite some time I used the Combine for a lot of different projects, such as the now defunct Safari research information service (again, I wrote a presentation of the service, but I'm sorry, it is in swedish).

Digital preservation and publication

Now my interests has shifted from the rather specific problems of indexing the web towards digital preservation, digital publishing and the searching of tagged text in general, and in particular SGML/XML documents. I do not feel that there is any difference between making available historical and current material (which would be preservation and publication, respectively). The main difference is the tense. What we do today (present tense) will in the future be a preservation problem (future tense).

The best example of my work in this area is the S:t Laurentius digital manuscript library.

I have written a conceptual description of the search engine we built for our manuscript collection.

The most importan current project within this area is Ediffah, which aims at implementing a search engine for EAD finding aids for archives in Swedish library collections.

Electronic publishing

Putting stuff on the Web

Today electronic publishing usually means publishing stuff on a web server. A better part of my time is spent on building infrastructure for creating facilities for just that, including cataloging of such stuff and delivering it to end users in a structured way. I've recently made two services for use within Lund university, Xerxes and Scripta Academica Lundensis, aimed for publishing and dissemination of undergraduate and postgraduate electronic theses and dissertations.

Printing and publishing

One of my passions is electronic typesetting, and in particular of scientific texts -- with equations, graphs, references, footnotes, quotations and you name it. Since I left science, I've had little opportunity to exercise that passion. However with the current XML boom, it is possible for me to make use of this old knowledge, in a new context.

For texts I write myself, I use the TEI XML dtd which I may then transformed into other formats (e.g., HTML) using XSLT. For printed output I translate it into troff source, which I format using with James Clark's groff package, a free re-engineered version of troff, the UNIX (TM) typesetting system. Throught the years, I have written a lot of XSLT scripts, *roff macros, dozens of perl scripts that work with *roff for various purposes:

I have prepared a worked example on how to format a TEI document for printing, using XSLT and groff. Provided that you have perl with XML::LibXSLT, a modern groff (version 1.18), TeX fonts, dvips and ps2pdf installed, you should be able to run this and get a laurentius.pdf from the laurentius.xml. Most of these things is available as standard on a decent Linux workstation.

I once wrote a gnuplot driver for producing graphs using *roff. Supports gpic and pic (vanilla UNIX version). My original driver seems obsolete now, but the current pic gnuplot driver doesn't seem to work with 3D graphds.
