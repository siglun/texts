Kaleidakustikon (i)

  • Written by Friedrich Kuhlau around 1820
  • An aleatoric composition for piano
  • Simple three-part structure (denoted capital A, B & C), in F major, F major or F minor, and E flat respectively

Kaleidakustikon (ii)

  • Consists of 21 piles of cards (named lower-case a to v, excluding j)
  • Each pile contains 11 cards numbered 2 to 12 (except h which only contains 3)
  • Each card contains one or two measures
  • Modern edition exists in Gorm Busk & Jan Maegaard (1996) Kuhlau kanons
  • The cards as presented by Busk & Maegaard

Why writing an application for it?

  • Show the music as one-armed bandit
  • Users could listen to the generated tunes in midi
  • Let users down-load waltzes as PDF
  • In short: Cool proof of concept...

Yeah, cool, but what's the concept?

  • We wanted a project where we could encode music in an abstract symbolic notation.
  • At the moment we are much into Music Encoding Initiative (MEI).
  • If we can present aleatoric music, we should be able use the technologies for philological purposes.
  • Need a technology stack for storage, retrieval and transformation of the cards.
  • That stack is similar to what is required for handling variants and sources.

Now, lets take a look at the data

  • Each layer has been transcribed.
  • The transcriptions can be presented as pdf
  • and as midi.
  • The transcription is made in MEI
  • In HTML5 canvas using mei2vexflow

How can we use the data?

  • Obviously, make pdf & midi.
  • We've stored the data in a database
  • Select cards/"divs" and make new tunes based on the selection..

The problem

  • F major vs F minor
  • I'll just write a script that adds a natural to all E notes part B if and only if card h changes the key to F minor.
