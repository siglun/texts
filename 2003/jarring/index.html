Digital preservation and accessiblity of Central
Asian Rare Books and Manuscripts

Background

The late Swedish diplomat Gunnar Jarring (1907-2002) was beside his career as a diplomate (as such he became UN secretary general's special envoy to the middle east) a turkologist and a specialist in Uighur literature.

As a globetrotter and diplomat he was able to aquire large collections of books and manuscripts from Central Asia (mainly the Uzbekistan, Kirghizistan and the Xinjiang province in China). 1982 he donated a manuscript collection to Lund university library. It comprises 560 volumes, and is held to be the third largest collection of Uighur manuscripts in the world.

About 75% of the collection is in the Uyghur language, the rest is in Chagatai (a now extinct language), Persian and Arabic. As of the age of the manuscripts, a majority of them are 19th century (please note that the first printer's workshop in Central Asia was established by Swedish missionaries in the early 20th century). The rest are from the 16th, 17th and 18th centuries. Jarring himself wrote a preliminary catalogue the collection.

The catalogue is handwritten in English with incipits and explicits in the languages of the objects. Ehrensvärd and Ekström provide a detailed description of the collection[1].

In his donation, Jarring expressed the wish that the catalogue should be revised and printed. We would also want to make the manuscripts available in digital form in a service similar to the one we already provide for our medieval manuscripts[2].

A digital library?

In order to create a digital library of this collection, we need collaboration with scholars from Central Asia. Both parties could benefit from such a project, we can trade digital library skills and technologies agains lingustic expertise. The overall goal would be to establish a digital library of cultural heritage resources in Uighur and other Altaic languages, such as Uzbek, of Central Asia. Such a resource would be valuable resource for researchers and students of Turkology worldwide.

We envisage that such a joint project between Lund university and institutions there could include the following:

  • Provide training of scholars from Central Asia in text encoding and the building of digital preservation services
  • Completion and XML encoding of Jarring's catalogue for the Lund collection
  • To catalogue similar collections in Central Asia
  • Digitization of the Lund collection and similar collections in Central Asia.
  • Form a "union catalogue" of digitized manuscripts and rare books in Uighur and other Altaic languages
