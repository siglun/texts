Are the Ways of Seeing Franscesca Woodman and Edith Gowin the same?

Sigfrid Lundberg's Stuff 2015-10-04

Francesca Woodman took her life 1981 at 22 years of age. By then she had become was a serious, hardworking and extremely good artist and photographer. She had started her exploration of art photography when she was 13. Here we are talking of a serious endeavour of a precosious, talented teenager, not a child's play. Much of her work is self portraiture, and she portrays herself naked.

There are several female photographers who have earned well-deserved fame for (among other thing) photographing naked humans, such as Imogen Cunningham and Ruth Bernard. They both depicted naked women, but as far as I know, neither Cunningham nor Bernard portrayed themselves, with or without clothes.

According to an interview with the curator, Anna Tellgren the reason why Woodman used herself as model was just practical. She was there we she needed it. The cost was lower and there were no problems with model release contracts.

I am sure there is more to than that, and it seems to me that the interest in this kind of self-portraiture has increased the last 10 years or so.

The only things I know about Edith Gowin is that she is a beautiful woman and that her husband is photographer Emmet Gowin. He is a wellknown photographer but almost certainly best known for his portraits of his often scantily clad or naked wife. The two have now celebrated their golden wedding anniversary since several years. In the interviews you find of them (for example) YouTube they seem to be still a loving couple. However, it might be that Emmet don't take as many photographs of Edith now as he did 40-50 years ago.

Many men, photographers or not, take pictures of naked women, often their wives. Some of have earned well-deserved fame.

There are photos taken by husband photographers of their wives that go far beyond the average such as Alfred Stieglitz (1864-1946) potraits of Georgia O'Keeffe (1887-1986). Edward Weston shot countless of nudes, of which, according to Robert Adams, most are fairly uninteresting:

With the exception of two fullength nudes of Tina Modotti and five of Charis Wilson in the Oceano dunes (not many, considering the number of nudes Weston took), the pictures that supposedly resulted from Weston's love for his subjects are relative to the rest of his life's work, unsuccesful; they are cold to the point of being dead.

Many photos of lesser artists than Weston deserve even less praise, and perhaps most such pictures shouldn't really have been taken.

One of the best, earliest and still extremely influential theoretical analyses of the nude in western art is in Ways of seeing by John Berger1.

His arguments can be summarized in contrasting statements. For example:

Men survey Women are the surveyed p. 46
Men act Women appear p. 47
Men look at women. Women watch themselves being looked at. p. 47
To be naked is to be oneself To be nude is to be seen as naked by others and yet not recognized for oneself p. 54

Foot notes

  1. Stieglitz And O'Keeffe: Their Love And Life In Letters
  2. Adams, Robert, 1994. Why people photograph. Aperture Foundation, New York.
  3. Berger, John, 1972. Ways of Seeing. Penguin Books ltd. London.
