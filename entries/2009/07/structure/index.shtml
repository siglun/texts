The description and encoding of structure

Sigfrid Lundberg's Stuff 2009-07-17

Many things in life can best be represented as trees. Indeed, life itself is a tree of various life forms. We have the animal and plant kingdoms, and these main categories are further sub-divided into phylae, classes, genera. The animal kingdom is divided into invertebrates and vertebrates. The latter include amphibians, reptiles, birds and mammals. Human knowledge itself often represented as a tree.

A lot of other things are trees; philosophers, mathematicins and computer scientists has spent efforts on how to model stuff as trees. People within library & information science have used the term classification systems for centuries, I suppose, and more recently we have started to talk about ontologies and knowledge organization. There are a lot of theory and a lot of standards in this area. Systems like topic maps (see also topicmaps.org) and more recently Simple Knowledge Organization System SKOS) are available for those who need a standard for knowledge organization.

On a very down to earth level, I tend to think of vocabularies as filters and navigators. The latter are trees and may contain many terms to an arbitrary depth. The filters are more like traditional facets. Think of Yahoo in the nineties. You could browse into Tuscany and get a very long list of resources. Then there was a bar at the top where you could choose between "Shopping", "Eating out", "Accomodation", "Travel" and so forth. These are broader categorizations that apply to Firenze as well as Siena or any place you might want to visit. We in the Web business call them filters or facets. The navigators are taxonomies in a broad sense.

From a user perspective, you have to take into account that there are two cognitive processes involved when using services that employ filters and navigators. The first one is much more fundamental, and is by nurture and possibly nature a part of the human mind: This is to say: Ah Tuscany, that is a part of Italy, isn't it. It's like distinguishing between a wasp and a bumble bee. On an evolutionary time-scale we've benefitted from being able to say, this is edible this is not, or this one has a sting and is likely to use it. This is to recognize something when you see it. The other way around is much more difficult: I want to know something about Tuscany: Ok, I click on Italy. You have to know more to begin with. Knowledge that might not be trivial. Such as that frogs are amphibians and that diabetes is an endocrinological disorder. On the other hand, you'll need to know such things in order to search successfully in Google as well.

There are structures that are inside a resource, and that require navigation as well. As a first approximation we can assume that any digital resource, such as digital resource such as an electronic book is an ordered hierarchy of content objects. That view has been questioned, and it is for instance claimed that just an ordinary text consists of many, overlapping hierarchies.
