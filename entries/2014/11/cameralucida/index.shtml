Camera Lucida and a tale on two mothers

Sigfrid Lundberg's Stuff 2014-11-04

By Sigfrid Lundberg, on Flickr

The ubiquitous photographers tries to prove that they've seen whatever there is to see outside Tivoli, Copenhagen, May 2012.

Camera Lucida is about Roland Barthes' thoughts about photographs and in particular the photographs mother and of her he had after her death. It is also about our memories.

It is just 120 pages, short book. In chapter 47 (out of 48 brief texts) he concludes:

The noeme [i.e., the essence] of photography is simple, banal; no depth: ‘That has been.’ I know our critics: What a whole book (even a short one) to discover something I knew at first glance?

Barthes is trying to pinpoint the essence of the photograph, not necessarily the essence of photography. He arrives at the conclusion that a photograph is basically an indication that its subject has existed and the photographer was there to see it. All those people who take photographs everywhere basically tells everyone that they've been there and they saw it whatever that was.

I think that he is basically correct. Most photographs taken are snaps, and the popularity of Instagram and Snapchat are further proof of that. In the very last chapter he says something really suprising:

Photography can in fact be an art: when there is no longer any madness in it, when its noeme is forgotten and when consequently its essence no longer acts on me
My mother teaches me riding my bike

I think my mother taught me riding my bike 1962 when I was six years old. This photo should be more than fifty.

Basically, Roland Barthes seem to say that art photography is boring. That is a very sweeping conclusion, and extremely subjective one. The artsy photos is pure studium, not punctum. The former means that the cognitive effect of an image is that one looks and perhaps learns and understand something from it, and the latter is another is an area in it which triggering a minor shock or surprise.

For me it was impossible to read it without thinking about mother, my own mother.
